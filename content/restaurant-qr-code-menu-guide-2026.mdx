A QR code menu eliminates that cycle. You print the code once—on a table tent, a card, or a sticker. The code points to your digital menu. When you update the PDF or webpage, every table gets the new version. No reprints. No waiting for the designer. No landfill.

## Design for Appetites

Your QR code doesn't have to look like a black-and-white checkerboard. Color matters in hospitality. Warm colors—red, orange, yellow—stimulate hunger. They signal comfort and energy. A QR code in your brand colors can feel more like part of the table than a tech add-on. Guests expect restaurants to feel intentional. A generic black square on a white card says "we added this because we had to." A branded code says "we thought about every detail."

Use our [free QR code generator](/) and experiment with the color customization. Match the code to your logo, your napkins, or your walls. A wine bar might go dark burgundy on cream. A brunch spot might try sunny yellow on white. As long as contrast stays high (dark on light, or light on dark), the code will scan reliably.

The goal: make the QR code look like it belongs. When it fits the room, guests treat it as part of the experience, not a compromise.

## Table Placement Strategy

Where you put the code matters as much as what it looks like. Guests scan from across the table, from the side, sometimes at an angle. The code needs to be visible, reachable, and protected.

**Lamination is non-negotiable.** Spills, grease, and wear destroy paper. Laminate the table tent or card. Use a matte finish to reduce glare—glossy surfaces reflect light and kill scans. A dollar per tent in lamination saves you from reprinting when someone knocks over a water glass.

**Placement rules:**
- **Table tents:** Center of the table or where the guest's eyes land when they sit. Avoid the edge; it gets bumped and bent. Angled tents work well—they face the guest without blocking conversation.
- **Standing cards:** Near the condiments, the salt, or the napkin holder—anywhere the guest already looks. Group them with other table elements so they feel integrated.
- **Counters:** Where people queue. Larger codes (3+ inches) work here because the scan distance is greater. Window and door displays need even bigger codes for sidewalk traffic.

## Scannability in Low Light

Most restaurant QR guides assume bright, even lighting. Bars and bistros don't have that. Candlelight, dimmer switches, and mood lighting create a scan nightmare if you don't plan for it. This is the section most blogs skip—and it's where many QR menu rollouts fail. A code that works at noon in the window seat might vanish at 8 p.m. in a corner booth.

**Contrast is king.** In low light, the camera needs clear separation between the code and the background. Black on white still wins. If you use brand colors, go dark modules on a light background—never the reverse. Light gray on white or dark gray on black will fail when the lights go down.

**Size up.** In dim rooms, phones struggle with small codes. Aim for at least 2.5 inches per side on table tents. For standing cards or bar menus, 3 inches or more. The extra real estate costs almost nothing; a failed scan costs you an order. When in doubt, go bigger. A slightly oversized code still looks professional. An undersized one that won't scan looks broken.

**Avoid glare.** Matte laminate. No glossy inserts. Keep the code away from direct overhead lights that create hotspots. Test in your actual space at your actual service lighting. If your staff can barely read the specials, guests will struggle to scan. Run through your busiest tables during dinner service. Scan from a seated position. Note which tables work and which don't. Adjust placement or size before your next print run.

## FAQ

**Do I need an app to read a menu QR?**  
No. Every smartphone camera reads QR codes natively. Guests point, scan, and the menu opens in the browser. No app download required.

**How do I update my menu prices without changing the QR?**  
If your QR code points to a hosted PDF or webpage, you update the file or page—not the code. The URL stays the same, so the QR code stays the same. Re-upload the PDF or edit the page, and every table gets the new menu.

**Is a PDF menu better than a website menu?**  
It depends. A PDF is easier: design once, upload, done. A webpage can load faster and adapt to screen size, but it requires more setup. For most restaurants, a well-designed PDF hosted on Google Drive, Dropbox, or your website is the simplest and most reliable option.
